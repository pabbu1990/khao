# How to Start a Ghost Kitchen or Home Food Business in Ontario (2026)

*A practical, no-fluff guide for anyone who wants to sell food they make — whether you cook biryani, jerk chicken, tamales, meal-prep boxes, or sourdough. Written by the team at [Khao](https://thekhao.com).*

---

You can cook. People keep telling you to "sell this." The hard part was never the food — it's everything around it: the rules, the orders, getting paid, and finding customers without a marketplace taking a 30% cut.

Here's the whole path, start to finish.

## Step 1 — Decide what you sell and who you feed

Don't start with "I'll cook everything." Start narrow. The kitchens that take off pick **one thing they're known for** and **one type of customer**:

- A cuisine or category (South Indian tiffins, Caribbean plates, keto meal-prep, baked goods, momos).
- A customer (busy office workers, students, new parents, gym-goers, your own community).
- A rhythm (weekday lunches, weekend specials, monthly pre-orders).

Narrow is easier to market, easier to cook at volume, and easier to become "the person who makes the best ___."

## Step 2 — Know the rules before you sell a single plate

This is the step most people skip — and it's the one that can shut you down. In Ontario, anyone selling food to the public falls under the **Food Premises Regulation (O. Reg. 493/17)** under the Health Protection and Promotion Act. The big question is whether your food is *low-risk* or *higher-risk*.

**Low-risk foods** (non-hazardous, shelf-stable, no refrigeration): most breads and baked goods, cookies, brownies, muffins, chocolate, hard candy, jams, pickles, preserves, granola, trail mix. Since a 2020 change to the regulation, home kitchens making **only** low-risk foods are exempt from some requirements (like food-handler certification and commercial dishwashing rules). You still may need to register with and be inspected by your local public health unit.

**Higher-risk foods** (anything needing temperature control — most full meals, meat and dairy dishes, curries, rice plates, most tiffin and meal-prep): these are **not** exempt. They generally need to be made in an **inspected, approved kitchen** — which usually means a licensed **commissary / shared commercial kitchen**, not your home stove. (More on that in Step 4.)

**Do this:** call your local public health unit before you start. They'll tell you exactly what applies to *your* menu.

- Ottawa: Ottawa Public Health
- Eastern Ontario: Eastern Ontario Health Unit (EOHU)
- GTA: Toronto Public Health / Peel / York / Halton

> This guide is general information, not legal advice. Food rules vary by what you make and where you live — confirm with your public health unit.

## Step 3 — Price it so you actually make money

A common rookie mistake: pricing off ingredient cost only. Real cost includes ingredients, packaging, gas/electricity, your time, and kitchen rental if you use one. A simple starting framework:

- **Food cost** should land around 28–35% of your price.
- Add packaging, fuel, and delivery.
- **Pay yourself** for prep and cook time — if you don't, you've built a job that loses money.
- Then sanity-check against what similar cooks charge.

If the math only works at a price customers won't pay, change the menu — not your wage.

## Step 4 — Set up your kitchen (home vs. commissary)

- **Low-risk menu?** You can likely run from your home kitchen (confirm with public health).
- **Full meals / higher-risk menu?** You'll most likely need a **shared commercial / commissary kitchen** — a licensed space you rent by the hour or day. It keeps you legal, lets you cook at volume, and many cooks find it cheaper than they expect.

In Ottawa, options include Canotek Kitchen, Harvest Commercial Kitchen, The Cauldron Kitchen, The Shared Commercial Kitchen, Partake Collective, and Delights by Mama (Stittsville). Most offer hourly rental and a path to your own food-premises approval.

## Step 5 — Take orders without chasing chats

Here's where most home kitchens drown. Orders come in over WhatsApp, Instagram DMs, texts, and "can you add one more?" messages — and you're scrolling three apps at 9pm trying to remember who ordered what.

You have three options:

1. **Keep doing DMs** — free, but it doesn't scale past a handful of orders and mistakes creep in.
2. **List on a marketplace** (Uber Eats / DoorDash) — they bring some discovery, but take **~20–30% commission**, own your customer relationship, and most require a licensed commercial kitchen anyway.
3. **Get your own ordering page** — a single link customers tap to see your menu and place an order, with everything landing in one organized list.

## Step 6 — Get your first 10 customers

You don't need an audience — you need ten orders. Where they come from:

- **Your existing chats and groups** (WhatsApp, Facebook, community groups) — post your menu + ordering link. This is your warmest channel.
- **Your community** — coworkers, your apartment building, your gym, parents at school, your place of worship.
- **A QR code** on a flyer at a commissary kitchen, community board, or grocery store.
- **One great dish photo** posted weekly on Instagram, with the order link in your bio.

Ask every happy customer for two things: a reorder, and one referral. That's your whole growth engine early on.

## Step 7 — Turn first-timers into regulars

Repeat customers are the entire business. After someone orders:

- Deliver a little faster or warmer than they expected.
- Drop a thank-you note or a small extra.
- Post next week's menu and tag the people who ordered.
- Make reordering one tap (your ordering link, every week).

A kitchen with 30 weekly regulars beats one chasing 300 strangers.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Selling higher-risk food from an unapproved home kitchen (a health complaint can end it).
- Pricing without paying yourself.
- Spreading across five cuisines instead of nailing one.
- Running everything through DMs until you burn out.
- Building on a marketplace that owns your customers and takes a third of every order.
